AI Mentions Monitor

Lessons learned, and step-by-step instructions for communications professionals

As a PR professional for a real estate brand in Canada, I have to keep track of our brand's visibility. I am regularly contacted by software providers offering solutions to track our "Generative Engine Optimization" – GEO (sometimes Answer Engine Optimization, or AEO), which monitors how and when your brand is mentioned when questions are asked of AI instead of regular search engines (or are answered by AI). Instead of paying for this service, I experimented with building my own dashboard. After a lot of back and forth and lessons learned in hindsight, I eventually had success and I'm pleased with the results. I wanted to share my learnings so that other communications professionals can create their own.

Here's how I used AI (Claude in this case) to build a dashboard that tracks how often a brand is mentioned when people ask AI models questions about a service or industry — and how that compares to competitors' mentions. Results are exportable as a shareable report.

What I wanted to build (experimentally, to start with)

Using a simple prompt in my AI tool (Claude, you can use others), I asked for a GEO monitor in dashboard form, presented in interactive HTML format. I was already familiar with hosting simple HTML pages on GitHub, so this didn't need to be added to a website – it's a standalone page. (For those unfamiliar, your AI will talk you through this process separately.)

I wanted to be able to routinely ask a list of the questions that our users and prospects would ask in natural language, and have the monitor show me whether our brand name was served up in an answer by any or all the key AI tools, which I'll refer to as LLMs (large language models) or "models". I chose to limit my searches to ChatGPT, Google Gemini, Claude, and Perplexity. I learned early that Google or Bing AI Overviews work on a different system and could not be included in these results – this monitor can only measure what happens when users directly query one of these LLMs, not just search on Google or Bing.

I knew my list of questions would be large, as I had eight questions that a user/prospect might typically ask about where to find sources of real estate data, and I wanted to apply each of those to six key geographic areas. For example, "Where can I find data on recent home sales [in Vancouver]?" So, those eight questions became 48. I also added a couple of national-only questions, and all those questions have to be asked of four separate LLMs, so that's around 200 individual queries – far more than I could ask manually.

I gave instructions to build a results page that showed where our brand did and didn't show up for each of those queries ("Mentions" vs "Missed") and display a trend graph over time so that we could map improvements or declines.

I told Claude that I wanted the dashboard results to be filterable by region and by LLM, so that I can see what users/prospects in Vancouver see when asking ChatGPT, versus Toronto users asking Gemini, for example.

I also wanted the monitor to report when our competitors' brands showed up in those same search results, and give me a separate colour-coded bar chart of those competitor mentions. As those results came from the same queries, there were no additional queries added, just a new way of parsing the results.

What we needed (that I didn't account for)

I didn't know that Claude couldn't simply "ask" these questions of the various LLMs, and give me the results. It would require API connections, for which I would need API keys – a code that Claude would add to the HTML code it was building, to plug in to the LLMs on the back end. I had to go to the developer portal site for each LLM and get these, which was easy enough.

I also didn't know that – even though you can get API keys for free, and the LLM accounts are free – using APIs requires tokens. These are very, very cheap (fractions of a cent each time) but not free after a handful of uses. I had to add my credit card to each LLM account and give each one $5 of credit to use. Hopefully that lasts for several months, when running 200-ish queries once a month!

I also didn't know about proxy servers, and had to learn fast. Short version for those unfamiliar: this monitor lives and runs in a local folder on my laptop, and it's only the results page that I download as a new HTML file, upload to GitHub, and share with my team. Your local laptop browser has a security rule that prevents it from talking directly to external APIs. You need a small program called a proxy server — think of it as a middleman that sits on your computer and routes the requests properly. This needs to be up and running before you hit "Run Scan" on your monitor, otherwise it won't connect. Instructions for all this are included in the full set of instructions.

What we successfully built (after a lot of wrong turns)

We ended up with a multi-tab monitor that displays results in a single-page HTML interactive dashboard. The tabs in the monitor are Results, Queries (full list, editable), Cities (editable list for when we expand geographically), Trends, and Models/Keys. There's a "Run Scan" button that starts the querying process. It takes about 10 minutes to run all 200-ish queries. There's a "Publish Results" button that I hit when it's complete.

The results are displayed in a single HTML page that shows the latest scan's data in big numbers at the top, then a trends over time graph, then a competitor results graph. Below that are filter buttons to isolate results by geographic region and/or by LLM. Below that are all the results from each query, expandable to see the summary of the AI-served answer.

I save this results HTML file to my laptop and then upload it to a hosting site (GitHub in my case – ask your AI for how to host a standalone HTML file) and then I have a publishable URL to share the interactive results page with my team. My process is to embed this in a centrally hosted document, with "how to use" instructions at the top explaining the filters, expandable sections, etc., and sharing the link to that.

Limitations of the Monitor

We cannot measure the accuracy of the AI responses. What we know is that the responses reflect what those specific AI models said, to those specific queries, to those specific users, in a specific location, on that specific day. LLM outputs are inherently non-deterministic, which means the same query can get a different answer on different days. We're not claiming to capture every possible AI interaction involving our brand. We also can't detect when an AI model hallucinates a mention of our brand — or incorrectly omits one, or says something incorrect about our brand.

What we're doing is capturing a consistent, repeatable sample over time. The value is in the trend, in comparisons over time and against competitors, not in the absolute number or as a precise audit. Also, remember that the queries you input don't have to include every possible wording of that question - LLMs are smart enough to figure out the variants.

In the process of doing monthly spot-checks (per the instructions) on the results of a scan, you may find that asking the same question directly of an LLM could bring up a different result. In those instances, it can be hard to use the monitor's results to identify next steps.

For example, if you see that ChatGTP is excluding your brand from results of a key question, you might want to run that question in ChatGPT yourself to see where it is pulling its answers from. In doing so, it might mention your brand this time, when queried directly, or offer a different mention. Most of the time, you will get a similar result to the monitor, and you can explore where there might be an opportunity to improve. But, again, this monitor is more about overall directional trends and relative competitive performance than it is about identifying exact numbers and opportunities.

Key lessons learned (mostly the hard way)

Lesson 01

Billing first, building second

Every AI provider requires payment before the API works reliably. Confirm it on all platforms before writing a single line of code.

Lesson 02

Finalize your queries with SEO before building

You can't meaningfully compare data from different query sets over time.

Lesson 03

Brief Claude like you'd brief a designer

Specific briefs produce specific results. Vague briefs produce vague results and many rounds of rework.

Lesson 04

AI model names and versions change regularly

Verify them from official docs. What worked last month may not work today, and the error won't tell you why. You will likely need to keep updating these, but your AI will guide you through it.

Lesson 05

Using a proxy is unavoidable but simple

Once it's running, you don't need to think about it again — but you do need to restart it every time you update the dashboard file.

Lesson 06

Experiment with one LLM, confirm it works, then add others

Adding complexity before the basics are stable makes debugging much harder.

Lesson 07

Competitor tracking costs nothing extra

The dashboard reads each AI response for multiple brand names simultaneously — no additional API calls, no additional cost.

Lesson 08

Clear your results history if you change your setup/queries/LLMs significantly

Scan data stored before a major change won't have the same structure or results as new data, which causes confusing gaps and inconsistencies in your charts.
